National Ukrainian cuisine

Ukrainian cuisine is known for its delicious tastes, traditional cooking technologies and generous ways to serve guests. It is an important part of the country’s culture and lifestyle. Yonderbound’s global ranking put Ukraine among the 20 best countries in the world for food. Ukrainian dishes are known for their variety and high taste qualities. Some Ukrainian dishes have a centuries-old history, for example, borscht, salo, varenyky.
Ukrainian cuisine heavily influenced by the rich dark soil (chernozem) from which its ingredients come and often involves many components.
The cuisine emphasizes the importance of wheat in particular, and grain in general, as the country is often referred to as the "breadbasket of Europe". The majority of Ukrainian dishes descend from ancient peasant dishes based on plentiful grain resources such as rye, as well as staple vegetables such as potato, cabbages, mushrooms and beetroots. Ukrainian dishes incorporate both traditional Slavic techniques as well as other European techniques, a byproduct of years of foreign jurisdiction and influence.
Here is a list of must try food in Ukraine that any foreigner should try to taste.

Borscht

Borscht, it refers to a type of Ukrainian beet soup known for its distinctive deep red color. It is the most famous dish in Ukrainian cuisine. Borscht, which is included on the list of Ukraine’s intangible heritage, has a centuries-old history and more than 70 recipes exist. The key ingredients of this hearty and rich Ukrainian soup are meat (usually beef or pork), beetroot and a variety of vegetables from beans to cabbage. It’s a sweet, tangy, and earthy soup that can vary in thickness and be served hot or cold, usually with a sour cream garnish and dill or garlic fritters called "pampushky".

Varenyky

Varenyky is the more commonly used name for pierogies in Ukraine. Varenyky are stuffed Ukrainian dumplings filled with a variety of ingredients like potatoes, sautéed cabbage, mushrooms, and cheese. They can also be made with dessert fillings like fruit and sweet cheese. The dumplings are typically formed into half-moon shapes before being boiled and served with "smetana" (literally sour cream). To Ukrainians, the varenyky dish isn’t just a national dish, it’s a symbol of their culture.

Holubtsi

Holubtsi refers to the stuffed cabbage rolls. Recipes for cabbage rolls vary but they typically consist of pickled cabbage leaves wrapped around a variety of meat or vegetarian fillings. A filling tipycally made with rice, but in same areas it is use a buckwheat groats or corn grits. The cereals are lightly cooked and combined with other ingredients like minced meat, pork cracklings, fried onions, and spices before being wrapped in the cabbage leaves. Depending on the season, cabbage leaves can be replaced with fresh beetroot leaves or young grape leaves. They can be made into different sizes and shapes and stewed in a variety of sauces and broths.

Salo

Salo refers to cured slabs of pork fatback. Salo is typically prepared by curing slabs of fat in salt and different spices and herbs like paprika, bay leaves, garlic, coriander, and black peppercorns. In some areas, it can also be smoked and aged. Salo can be eaten raw or cooked (salted, boiled, smoked, fried) and makes a great accompaniment to rye bread and horilka. So, not much to say here, except that salo is a famous dish of traditional Ukrainian cuisine and Ukrainians love their Salo, not only for its unique taste but also for the health benefits it brings. Pork fat is high in vitamin D and A, which helps with brain function, digestion, and detoxification.

Banosh

This silky Ukrainian corn porridge is not only a widely enjoyed dish across Ukraine, but it is also an essential part of the traditional cuisine of our nation. Originating in the Carpathian mountains, Banosh was once linked with the Hutsul minority, with males being assigned to make the meal. That is what they call a thick porridge made of corn flour and boiled in cream or sour cream. It is always sprinkled with brynza (a type of sheep milk cheese) and roasted bacon. Mushrooms are also often added to the porridge, to make the taste even richer. The Hutsuls say that real banosh should only be cooked in a cauldron on an open fire and you can stir it only with a wooden ladle.

Horseradish

Ukrainian horseradish is popular in two forms. First, it is a fresh plant that is grated and sprinkled over slices of meat and boiled eggs to add a special taste. Second, it is a main ingredient in the horseradish and beetroot salad, which is regarded as a sauce and consumed with all kinds of dishes: meat, kholodets, salo, etc.

Holodets

For Ukrainians, it is the central dish served at all celebrations. Holodets is made of meat broth, frozen to a jelly-like state, with pieces of meat inside. The dish’s name comes from the Ukrainian word holodnyii, which means “cold”. One of the main components for this kind of aspic is pork leg. To be more specific, the lowest part, the one that ends with hoofs.

Zelenyj Borscht

Zelenyj borscht translates to “green borscht” and refers to a type of sour-tasting Ukrainian soup made from leafy vegetables. Sorrel is most commonly used though it can be made with other vegetables as well like spinach, chard, nettle, and orache. Like beet-based borscht, zelenyj borscht is based on a meat or vegetable broth and typically served with boiled potatoes and hard-boiled eggs. It’s usually garnished with dill and served with sour cream.

Halushky

Halushky are small pieces of dough, round or rectangular, cooked in water or milk. A feature that sets them apart from varenyky is the absence of any filling. However, before serving, they are generously sprinkled with grilled pieces of lard or onion, or they are just added to soup. In Poltava, locals continued the tradition of perpetuating their favorite dish in stone - that is how a monument in the form of a huge plate with seventeen dumplings appeared.

Deruny

Deruny is the Ukrainian version of a potato pancake. First, the potatoes are grated into a homogenous mass. Then, the egg, flour, salt, and pepper are added to the mix, and the resulting mixture is fried in vegetable oil. Deruny in Ukraine are quite thin and crispy. Depending on the cook, other ingredients like ground meat, cabbage, and mushrooms may be added but sour cream accented with dill is a common accompaniment.

Syrnyky

Syrnyky is a traditional Ukranian dessert/breakfast dish that is made of soft home cheese, eggs, and some flour, then fried. More often than not people add raisins and vanilla extract. Syrnyky is supposed to be crispy outside and creamy inside. After being gently fried in a pan, syrniki is topped with jam and sour cream. It literally melts in the mouth and will fast become your favorite dish.

Pampushky

Pampushky is the epitome of garlic bread! With a soft pillowy texture and flavorful garlic toppings. These lovely yeasted buns from Ukraine come in both sweet and savory varieties. The sweet form is frequently loaded with jam, berries, cottage cheese, and fresh fruit, while the savory variant is usually seasoned with garlic sauce and eaten with Borscht (Ukrainian beet soup). German colonists may have been the ones who brought Pampushky to Ukraine. These delicious buns became a popular national dish at the end of the 19th century, eventually gaining “status” as a typical Ukrainian dish.

Kulesha

The Ukrainians use cornflour, butter, salt, and water to make this dish. Once ready to eat, it will be sliced with a knife and served with Bryndza cheese, bacon, pork rinds, or mushrooms. This delicacy has several names. In the central area, it’s known as Mamalyga, but the Carpathians people call it Kulesha. Despite the name difference in different regions, the recipe for this Ukrainian corn meal mush remains the same.

Kapusniak

Kapusniak is one of the oldest and significant dishes in Ukraine. It used to be prepared for weddings, funerals, and Christmas dinners. Nowadays, Ukrainians enjoy this soup or even in a form of porridge (called "zasypana kapusta", literally - filled up cabbage) throughout the year. The key ingredient of kapusniak is sauerkraut, which is thoroughly washed before cooking to give the soup its transparent color. It is cooked in meat or mushroom broth with potatoes, onions, carrots, and bay leaves. Traditionally kapusniak is served with sour cream and chopped parsley. If you are preparing "zasypana kapusta", after the cabbage has been steamed, it is covered with millet and seasoned with melted lard. This is another unforgettable national dish, which combines the taste of stewed cabbage with the taste of fried pieces of lard ("shkvarky").
